What are Capital Markets?

Definition

A capital market is a place that allows the trading of funding instruments such as shares, debentures, debt instruments, bonds, ETFs, etc. It is a source for raising funds for individuals, firms, and governments.

Capital markets refer to markets of long-term securities investment which comprise both public and private debt instruments with maturity more than 1 year or no fixed maturity period.

Functions

  • To fulfill the need of surplus unit by offering various investment tools
  • To provide a wide variety of instruments to the surplus unit to diversify their risk
  • To provide intermediary services to raise funds for corporate investment and expansion
  • To assist the process of economic development
  • To promote private enterprise – diversification of Malaysian economy

Types of Securities

Capital Market
Equity Market Trading common stocks and preferred stocks
Bond Market Corporations and governments can raise funds by issuing private and government debt securities

The capital market is broadly divided into two main segments: the equity market and the bond market. The equity market deals with ownership securities like stocks, while the bond market focuses on debt securities with longer-term maturities.

Equity Market

Stock Market Securities

Common Stock

Common stock represents the fundamental ownership claim in a public or private corporation. Dividend payments are discretionary and not guaranteed.

Key Characteristics:

  • Discretionary dividend payments
  • Residual claim status
  • Limited liability
  • Voting rights

Preferred Stock

Preferred stock is a type of equity that represents ownership in a company with higher claim on distributions than common stockholders.

Key Characteristics:

  • Priority over common stock in dividend payments
  • Fixed dividend payment (typically)
  • May have cumulative dividend features
  • Usually no voting rights (in most cases)

Primary vs. Secondary Stock Markets

Primary Stock Market

Primary markets are where corporations (fund users) raise funds through new issues of stocks, such as initial public offerings (IPOs). The new stock securities are sold to initial investors (fund suppliers).

Types of Underwriting:
  1. Commitment underwriting: Investment bank guarantees the corporation a price for newly issued stocks by buying the whole issue at a fixed price from the corporate issuer.
  2. Best efforts underwriting: Investment bank does not guarantee a price to the issuer. Acts like a placing/distribution agent for a fee.
  3. Syndicate underwriting: A group of investment banks help to sell and distribute a new issue.
Seasoned Offerings

Seasoned offerings are the sale of additional securities by a firm whose securities are currently publicly traded. Often, existing shareholders have pre-emptive rights to maintain their proportional ownership.

Rights offerings allow existing shareholders to purchase additional shares at slightly below market price, helping companies save on underwriting costs.

Secondary Stock Market

Secondary stock markets are where stocks, once issued, are traded, bought, and sold by investors. In Malaysia, Bursa Malaysia provides the platform for secondary market trading.

Types of Markets on Bursa Malaysia:
  • 1
    MAIN MARKET

    Primary market for larger companies with strong operating and profit track records. Minimum market capitalization: RM500 million.

  • 2
    ACE MARKET

    Smaller companies with strong growth potential. No minimum profit or operating track record required.

  • 3
    LEAP MARKET

    Underserved SMEs. No need to demonstrate operating or financial track record. Adviser-driven market only available to sophisticated investors.

Advantages and Risks of Stock Market Investment

Advantages

  • Capital Appreciation

    Potential for growth in share value over time, especially with well-performing companies.

  • Dividends

    Regular income from company profits distributed to shareholders.

  • Liquidity

    Ability to buy and sell shares quickly in the secondary market.

  • Diversification

    Ability to spread investments across different sectors and companies to reduce risk.

  • Ownership in Companies

    Partial ownership of businesses, including voting rights in company decisions.

  • Limited Liability

    Protection from personal liability beyond the amount invested.

Risks

  • Market Risk

    Risk of overall market decline affecting all investments regardless of individual company performance.

  • Company-specific Risk

    Risk related to the performance and prospects of individual companies.

  • Interest Rate Risk

    Changes in interest rates can affect stock prices, often negatively when rates rise.

  • Inflation Risk

    Risk that inflation will erode the real value of investment returns.

  • Liquidity Risk

    Risk of being unable to sell shares quickly without affecting the price, especially for smaller company stocks.

  • Political/Regulatory Risk

    Changes in government policy or regulations that might adversely affect companies or sectors.

  • Currency Risk

    For international investments, changes in exchange rates can affect returns.

Stock Market Indices

A stock market index is the composite value of a group of secondary market-traded stocks. It shows the movements in the stock market and provides investors with information on movement of a broader range of the secondary market.

Stock Market Structure

Primary Market Process

Initial Public Offering (IPO) Process

The IPO process allows companies to raise capital by offering shares to the public for the first time. This transition from private to public ownership involves several key steps:

  1. Company selects investment banks as underwriters
  2. Due diligence and regulatory filings are completed
  3. Pricing of the shares is determined
  4. Shares are allocated to initial investors
  5. Trading begins on the stock exchange

Key Participants in the Primary Market

Issuing Companies

Companies seeking to raise capital through the issuance of new securities

Investment Banks

Underwriters that help companies prepare and distribute securities to investors

Initial Investors

Individuals and institutions that purchase newly issued securities directly from underwriters

Electronic Order-Driven System

Bursa Malaysia operates on an electronic order-driven system where buy and sell orders are matched automatically based on price and time priority.

Trading Hours

Trading hours are divided into:

  • Pre-opening: 8:30 am - 9:00 am
  • Morning session: 9:00 am - 12:30 pm
  • Lunch break: 12:30 pm - 2:30 pm
  • Afternoon session: 2:30 pm - 5:00 pm
Market Participants

Trading is facilitated by licensed brokers who act as intermediaries between buyers and sellers. Other participants include market makers who provide liquidity.

Bond Market

What are Bonds?

Definition

Bonds are long-term debt obligations issued by corporations and governments. Proceeds from a bond issue are used to raise funds to support long-term operations of the issuers (example: for capital expenditure projects).

In return, the bond investors will receive a specified amount of coupon payments (if any) together with principal (the face value of the bond) at the end of the maturity period.

Types

Bonds can come in various forms:

  • Discount bonds
  • Fixed-rate bonds
  • Floating-rate bonds
  • Hybrid bonds
  • Sukuk (Islamic bonds)

Bond Issuance Process

Step 1: Bond Issuance

Issuers (corporations or governments) plan to borrow money from investors by issuing bonds.

Step 2: Capital Transfer

Investors (lenders) provide funds to the issuer in exchange for the bond.

Step 3: Regular Coupon Payments

The issuer makes periodic interest payments (coupons) to bondholders according to the agreed schedule and rate.

Step 4: Bond Maturity

Upon maturity, the issuer repays the principal amount (face value) to bondholders, along with the final interest payment if applicable.

Example Scenario

Firm A purchases a bond of RM1 million that matures on 1st Jan 2050 from a company at a coupon rate of 3% per annum.

  • • Firm A is now an institutional bondholder.
  • • Firm A will receive 3% interest payment on the face value from the company every year until maturity.
  • • The company will use the funds to finance their business while paying interest annually to Firm A.
  • • At maturity (2050), the company will pay Firm A the face value (RM1 million) along with the final interest payment.

Types of Bonds in Malaysia

Government Bonds

Malaysian Government Securities (MGS)

Conventional bonds issued by the Government of Malaysia with long-term maturities. Used to fund development expenditures.

Government Investment Issues (GII)

Islamic securities issued by the Government of Malaysia based on Shariah principles.

Malaysia Treasury Bills (MTB)

Short-term securities with maturities of less than a year, issued to meet the government's short-term financing needs.

Bank Negara Monetary Notes (BNMN)

Issued by Bank Negara Malaysia for monetary policy implementation and liquidity management.

Corporate Bonds

Private Debt Securities (PDS)

Conventional bonds issued by corporations to fund business operations, expansion, acquisitions, or refinancing.

Corporate Sukuk

Islamic bonds structured to comply with Shariah principles, issued by corporations to raise capital.

Asset-Backed Securities (ABS)

Securities backed by a pool of assets, such as loans, leases, or receivables.

Convertible Bonds

Bonds that can be converted into a specified number of the issuing company's shares at the bondholder's option.

Bond Secondary Market

Bonds and sukuk can be bought and sold in the secondary bond market after they have been issued in the primary market. In Malaysia, the secondary market has specific characteristics:

Trading Venues

  • Retail bonds and sukuk may be issued and traded either on Bursa Malaysia or over-the-counter (OTC) through appointed banks.
  • Most secondary market activity in Malaysia is concentrated on Malaysian Government Securities (MGS).

Key Differences from Primary Market

  • Bond and sukuk sale proceeds go to the seller (either an investor or a dealer) rather than the issuer.
  • Prices are determined by market forces rather than the issuer.
  • Provides liquidity to bondholders who wish to sell before maturity.

    The date when the issuer will redeem the bond by repaying all the debt.

    Type of Issuer: Malaysia Government Securities (MGS)

    The issuer can be a corporation or government.

    For this bond, the issuer is the Government of Malaysia.

Premium, Discount and Par Bonds

Premium Bond

A premium bond occurs when the bond's calculated value (Vb) is greater than its par value.

Condition:

Coupon rate > Market yield to maturity

This means investors are willing to pay more than the face value because the bond's coupon rate is higher than what they could get on similar bonds in the current market.

Discount Bond

A discount bond occurs when the bond's calculated value (Vb) is lower than its par value.

Condition:

Coupon rate < Market yield to maturity

This means the bond is selling below face value because its stated interest rate is lower than what investors could get on new bonds in the current market.

Par Bond

A par bond occurs when the bond's calculated value (Vb) is equal to its par value.

Condition:

Coupon rate = Market yield to maturity

This means the bond's stated interest rate is exactly in line with the current market rates for similar bonds, so it trades at its face value.

Basic Risks in Bond Market

Interest Rate Risk

Risk that changes in interest rates will affect the market value of bonds (prices move inversely to interest rate changes).

Credit Risk

Risk that the bond issuer will default on its obligations to pay interest or repay principal.

Inflation Risk

Risk that inflation will erode the real value of bond returns as purchasing power decreases.

Liquidity Risk

Risk that a bond cannot be sold quickly at a reasonable price in the secondary market.

Reinvestment Risk

Risk that future coupon payments will need to be reinvested at lower interest rates.

Currency Risk

Risk from unfavorable exchange rate movements for bonds denominated in foreign currencies.

Market Risk

Risk that the overall bond market will decline, affecting all bonds regardless of individual characteristics.

Sovereign Risk

Risk associated with investing in government bonds due to political or economic instability.

Credit Ratings

Credit Ratings Explained

What are Credit Ratings?

Credit ratings are assessments conducted by recognized rating agencies to evaluate the credit quality of the issuer in terms of its ability to repay principal and make interest payments in full and on time.

Each rating agency has its own methodology in deriving their credit opinions and addressing specific rating scales for the bonds to be issued.

Rating Agencies

International Agencies:

  • • Standard & Poor's Global Ratings
  • • Moody's Investors Service
  • • Fitch Ratings

Malaysian Agencies:

  • • RAM Ratings
  • • Malaysian Rating Corporation Berhad (MARC)

Importance of Credit Ratings for Bonds

Risk Assessment

  • Corporate bonds have higher risk than government bonds
  • Bonds are typically backed by the issuer's ability to pay from earnings from future operations
  • Credit risk significantly affects bond pricing and attractiveness to investors

Market Impact

  • Higher ratings (lower risk) typically result in lower coupon rates for issuers
  • Lower ratings (higher risk) lead to higher coupon rates to attract investors
  • Bond price and yield in the secondary market are significantly affected by ratings
  • Rating changes can trigger major price movements in the bond market

Important Note:

Credit ratings address only credit risk. They do not consider other risks such as interest rate risk, liquidity risk, market risk, or other factors that may affect bond prices.

Investment Grade

Ratings from AAA to BBB- indicate relatively low to moderate credit risk. These issuers are considered to have adequate to excellent capacity to meet financial commitments.

Speculative Grade

Ratings from BB+ to B- indicate higher credit risk but still with capacity to meet financial commitments. These bonds typically offer higher yields to compensate for the additional risk.

Junk/Default

Ratings from CCC+ to D indicate high credit risk to actual default. These issuers are vulnerable or have already failed to meet financial commitments.

Rating Modifiers

  • S&P and Fitch modify ratings from 'AA' to 'CCC' by adding a plus (+) or minus (-) sign to show relative standing within major rating categories.
  • Moody's appends numerical modifiers 1, 2, and 3 from 'Aa' to 'Caa' to indicate position within the category (1 being the highest).
  • MARC may modify ratings from 'AA' to 'B' by adding a plus (+) or minus (-) sign.
  • RAM applies 1, 2, or 3 in each rating category from 'AA' to 'C' to show relative standing within the major rating categories.

Equity vs Bond Comparison

Test your understanding of the differences between equity and bond investments.

True or False:

1. Stocks represent ownership in a company.

2. Interest rates and bond prices have an inverse relationship.

3. Stocks have a fixed maturity date like bonds.

4. A zero-coupon bond pays interest to the bondholder at regular intervals.

5. Bond ratings are assigned to assess the risk of default by the issuer.

6. A company with a high bond rating is more likely to pay lower interest on its bonds.

7. The coupon rate of a bond remains constant, regardless of changes in the bond's price.

Answers:

  1. True. Stocks represent ownership (equity) in a company. When you buy shares of stock, you become a partial owner of the company.
  2. True. When interest rates rise, bond prices fall, and when interest rates fall, bond prices rise. This inverse relationship is a fundamental principle of bond investing.
  3. False. Unlike bonds, stocks do not have a fixed maturity date. They represent an ownership stake that continues indefinitely unless the company is acquired, goes private, or ceases to exist.
  4. False. A zero-coupon bond does not pay periodic interest. Instead, it is issued at a discount to its face value and matures at face value, with the difference representing the interest earned.
  5. True. Credit rating agencies assign bond ratings specifically to assess the creditworthiness of issuers and their ability to make timely interest and principal payments.
  6. True. Companies with higher bond ratings (better creditworthiness) are typically able to issue bonds with lower coupon rates because they represent lower default risk to investors.
  7. True. The coupon rate is fixed at issuance and does not change throughout the bond's life, regardless of changes in the bond's market price. What changes is the bond's yield to maturity, which reflects the current market price.
