Interest rates play a central role in our financial system and economy. They influence decisions made by individuals, businesses, and governments on a daily basis.

What is Interest Rate?

To a lender (surplus unit): The return earned for parting with funds over a period of time.

To a borrower (deficit unit): The price to pay over a specific period of time (the interest expenses).

Central Bank's Role

Central banks play an important role in stabilizing prices, output, the financial system, exchange rates, and interest rates.

They control the monetary base by:

  • Buying and selling assets (government securities)
  • Making loans to banks at interest rate corridors
  • Adjusting the mix of assets they hold

Significance of Interest Rates

Benchmark Setting

Sets benchmark for Average Overnight Interbank Rate (AOIR) and serves as a monetary policy tool.

Cost & Returns

Determines cost of borrowing for consumers and businesses, as well as returns on investments and savings.

Price Stability

Helps manage inflationary pressure - lower rates encourage spending while higher rates help control inflation.

FX Rates

Influences exchange rates between currencies - affects the value of Ringgit and can attract foreign capital.

Asset Pricing

Impacts the pricing of various assets like bonds, stocks, and real estate, affecting investment decisions.

Credit Growth

Higher rates restrict credit growth making it harder for businesses and households to get financing.

Types of Interest Rates

Nominal vs. Real Interest Rates

Nominal Interest Rate

The interest rate that you see advertised by banks and lenders. This is the actual percentage you earn or pay on a loan, but it doesn't account for inflation.

"This is the rate you see on bank signs and loan agreements."

Real Interest Rate

The nominal interest rate adjusted for inflation. This is the effective interest rate that represents the actual purchasing power you gain or lose.

"This shows the true economic value of interest when inflation is considered."

Converting Between Nominal and Real Rates

The Formula

Real Interest Rate = Nominal Interest Rate − Inflation Rate

This is a simple approximation formula that works well for relatively low inflation rates.

Example:

Suppose a bank wants to earn 10% interest, but it expects inflation to be 3% during the loan period.

  • • If the bank charged only 10% (not accounting for inflation), their real return would be just 7% (10% - 3%).
  • • To achieve a real return of 10%, the bank would need to charge a nominal interest rate of 13% (10% + 3%).

Real Interest Rate Calculator

The real interest rate is: 3.0%

Simple Interest Calculation

Simple Interest Formula

Simple Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Total Amount = Principal + (Principal × Rate × Time)

Example:

You have borrowed RM10,000 at a simple interest rate of 8% per annum for 3 years.

Principal = RM10,000

Rate = 8% per year = 0.08

Time = 3 years = 3

Interest = 10,000 × 0.08 × 3 = RM2,400

Total Repayment = RM10,000 + RM2,400 = RM12,400

Fixed vs. Variable Rates

Fixed Rate

An interest rate that does not change over the life of a loan; amount of monthly installment remains the same.

Advantage: Predictable payments make budgeting easier for customers

Disadvantage: Exposes banks to interest rate risk, especially when rates are volatile

Applications: Credit card facilities, car loans, hire purchase agreements

Variable Rate

An interest rate that fluctuates over time - known as Base Rate (BR) and Base Lending Rate (BLR).

Advantage: Protects both borrower and lender against interest rate fluctuations

Disadvantage: Can increase credit and collateral risk for borrowers

Applications: Property-based lending and business loans

Determinants of Interest Rates

Several key factors influence the level of interest rates in an economy. Understanding these determinants helps explain why rates fluctuate over time.

Inflation

Higher actual or expected inflation leads to higher interest rates (positive relationship).

Investors need to earn higher returns to compensate for the increased cost of foregoing consumption today only to buy more expensive goods in the future.

When inflation rises, central banks often raise interest rates to cool down the economy and control price increases.

Real Interest Rates

The rate that would exist on a security without any expected inflation over the holding period.

It measures consumers' relative time preference for consuming today rather than later - the higher the preference to consume today, the higher the real interest rate.

During economic growth, the rate of return on investments increases, leading to higher demand for loans and higher real interest rates.

Default Risk

The risk that the issuer will not pay the interest and principal on a timely basis.

Higher default risk means investors demand higher interest rates to compensate for the risk exposure.

This is why corporate bonds typically pay higher interest rates than government bonds, and why borrowers with poor credit scores pay higher rates.

Liquidity Risk

Liquidity Preference Theory suggests that investors should demand a higher interest rate on securities with long-term maturities that carry greater risk.

All other factors being equal, investors prefer cash or other highly liquid holdings.

When more people want to hold money for transactions (high liquidity preference), interest rates tend to rise.

Time to Maturity

Refers to the length of time until a security will mature, affecting interest rates through the 'term structure of interest rates' or 'yield curve'.

The change in required interest rates as maturity changes is called the 'maturity premium'.

The maturity premium can be positive (upward sloping yield curve), negative (downward sloping), or zero (flat yield curve).

Understanding Yield Curves

A yield curve shows the relationship between interest rates (or yields) and time to maturity for debt securities with equal credit quality.

Normal (Upward Sloping)

Longer-term bonds have higher yields than shorter-term ones. This is the most common shape, indicating economic expansion.

Inverted (Downward Sloping)

Short-term yields are higher than long-term yields. Often viewed as a predictor of economic recession.

Flat

Short and long-term yields are similar. May indicate economic transition, often between normal and inverted curves.

Policy Interest Rates

Central banks use policy interest rates as a key tool to influence the broader economy and achieve monetary policy objectives.

Bank Negara Malaysia's Influence on Interest Rates

Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) influences domestic interest rates primarily through the Overnight Policy Rate (OPR). The Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) of BNM decides whether the OPR is at an appropriate level.

The main objective of setting the OPR is to maintain price stability while giving due regard to developments in the economy.

What is the OPR?

The Overnight Policy Rate (OPR) is BNM's main reference rate in the financial market. It influences, among other things, banks' lending and financing rates, as well as deposit rates throughout the economy.

Impact of OPR Changes

When OPR Increases

  • Cost of borrowing increases for individuals and businesses

  • Higher returns on savings and investments

  • Reduces overall consumption and investment

  • Helps cool inflation by slowing economic activity

  • May strengthen the currency as foreign investment is attracted

When OPR Decreases

  • Lower borrowing costs make loans more affordable

  • Reduced returns on savings and deposits

  • Stimulates spending and investment

  • Can increase inflation if maintained for extended periods

  • May weaken currency relative to other currencies

Reference Rate Frameworks

Financial institutions use reference rates to price their lending products. Malaysia has seen several reference rate frameworks over the years.

OPR and Lending Rate Relationship

Changes in the Overnight Policy Rate (OPR) affect lending rates used by banks. Over time, Malaysia has used different reference rate frameworks.

Base Lending Rate (BLR)

Period: Before January 2, 2015

• Set by BNM based on cost of lending to financial institutions

• Computed based on 3-month weighted average interbank rate

• Included a flat administrative margin of 2.25 percentage points

• Linked to BNM's intervention rate (now known as OPR)

Base Rate (BR)

Period: January 2, 2015 - July 31, 2022

• Determined by financial institutions themselves

• Based on banks' cost of funds and SRR

• Also considered borrower credit risk, liquidity risk premium, operating costs, and profit margin

• More transparent pricing framework compared to BLR

Standardized Base Rate (SBR)

Period: From August 1, 2022

• Directly linked to the OPR

• SBR = OPR

• Lending/financing rate = SBR + spread

• Creates greater transparency and allows faster transmission of monetary policy

• Easier for consumers to compare loan products

Old Reference Rate Framework (BLR)

The Base Lending Rate (BLR) was computed based on the 3-month weighted average interbank rate of the previous month, and a flat administrative margin of 2.25 percentage points.

BLR Formula:

For Commercial Banks:

Computed BLR = Intervention rate / (1-SRR) × 0.8 + 2.25%

For Finance Companies:

Computed BLR = Intervention rate / (1-SRR) + 2.25%

Note: The "Intervention rate" is now known as the OPR

In this framework, banks would typically quote lending rates to customers as "BLR - x%" (for favorable borrowers) or "BLR + y%" (for higher risk borrowers), where x and y are the risk adjustments.

New Reference Rate Frameworks

Base Rate (BR) Framework

Effective January 2, 2015, Base Rate (BR) replaced the BLR as the main reference rate for new retail floating-rate loans. This framework allowed for more transparency and flexibility.

Under the BR system, the rate was determined by financial institutions themselves, taking into account:

  • Bank's cost of funds
  • Statutory Reserve Requirement (SRR)
  • Borrower credit risk
  • Liquidity risk premium
  • Operating costs
  • Profit margin

Standardized Base Rate (SBR) Framework

Effective August 1, 2022, the Standardized Base Rate (SBR) took effect as the reference rate for the pricing of new retail floating-rate loans and the refinancing of existing loans.

SBR Formula:

Standardized Base Rate (SBR) = Benchmark rate

Benchmark rate = OPR

Lending/financing rate = SBR + spread

Or, Lending/financing rate = OPR + spread

The SBR framework provides greater transparency as it directly links lending rates to the OPR. This makes it easier for borrowers to understand the impact of OPR changes on their loan repayments.

Comparison Example

Here's a comparison of how the different reference rate frameworks would apply to a sample loan:

Details BLR BR SBR
Loan Amount RM350,000 RM350,000 RM350,000
Tenure 30 years 30 years 30 years
Reference Rate BLR: 6.65% BR: 3.15% SBR: 3.00%
Spread/Risk Factor BLR - 2.10% BR + 1.40% SBR + 1.60%
Effective Interest Rate 4.55% 4.55% 4.60%

Key Differences:

  • Under BLR, the risk factor is deducted from the BLR (e.g., BLR - 2.1%)
  • Under BR and SBR, the spread is added to the reference rate (e.g., BR + 1.4%)
  • SBR creates consistency across all banks since it directly links to the OPR
  • With SBR, when OPR changes, the impact on loan rates is immediate and transparent

Impact on Consumers

For Existing Borrowers

  • Existing loans under BLR or BR will continue until refinanced or restructured

  • Changes in OPR still affect existing loans but the transmission mechanism might be different

  • Opportunity to refinance under SBR if terms are more favorable

For New Borrowers

  • All new floating-rate loans use the SBR framework

  • Greater transparency - easier to understand how OPR changes affect loan rates

  • Easier to compare loan offerings across different banks

  • Immediate impact of monetary policy changes on loan rates

Convert the following nominal interest rate to a real interest rate using the inflation rate:

%

Calculate the simple interest earned on a principal of RM10,000 for 3 years at an annual interest rate of 5%.

Your answer:

RM

Solution:

Using the formula: Simple Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Simple Interest = RM10,000 × 0.05 × 3 = RM1,500

Problem 3: Total Amount Due

Using the simple interest formula, calculate the total amount due for a RM5,000 loan at a 6% interest rate over 4 years.

Your answer:

RM

Solution:

First, calculate the interest: Simple Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Simple Interest = RM5,000 × 0.06 × 4 = RM1,200

Total Amount Due = Principal + Interest

Total Amount Due = RM5,000 + RM1,200 = RM6,200

True/False Questions

1. The Overnight Policy Rate (OPR) is set by the Malaysian government to control the exchange rate of the Malaysian Ringgit.

2. Inflation expectations have no impact on the decision-making of businesses and consumers in Malaysia.

3. The Kuala Lumpur Interbank Offered Rate (KLIBOR) is influenced solely by global economic conditions and has no connection to Malaysian economic factors.

4. An increase in the Overnight Policy Rate (OPR) would likely lead to a decrease in borrowing costs for businesses and individuals in Malaysia.

5. Bank Negara Malaysia's primary goal in adjusting interest rates is to maximize corporate profits and stock market performance.

Answers:

  1. False - The OPR is set by Bank Negara Malaysia (not the government) primarily to manage monetary policy and price stability, not directly control exchange rates.
  2. False - Inflation expectations significantly impact business and consumer decision-making regarding investments, savings, and spending.
  3. False - KLIBOR is heavily influenced by Malaysian economic factors, including BNM's monetary policy, domestic interest rates, and local banking conditions.
  4. False - An increase in OPR typically leads to an increase in borrowing costs as banks adjust their lending rates upward.
  5. False - BNM's primary goal in adjusting interest rates is to achieve price stability and support sustainable economic growth.
