4.1 Basics of Consumer Credit

What is Consumer Credit?

Consumer credit is the use of credit by individuals for personal or household purposes. It involves obtaining goods or services and paying for them at a later date under agreed upon terms.

Consumer credit helps individuals buy goods or services, cope with emergencies, but it also comes with costs and risks.

4.1.1 Importance of Credit Creation

Benefits of Credit

  • Enable purchase even when funds are low
  • A cushion for financial emergencies
  • Advance notice of sales
  • Easier to return merchandise
  • Increases the money supply, affecting economic activity
  • Promotes economic growth and enhances social welfare

Pitfalls of Credit

  • Temptation to overspend
  • Failure to repay loan may lead to loss of income
  • It does not increase total purchasing power
  • Credit costs money

4.1.2 Types and Products of Credit

Open-end credit allows borrowing up to a limit and repaying it flexibly. Examples include credit cards, personal lines of credit, and home equity lines of credit.

Credit Card

A payment card that allows you to borrow money to make purchases. You must repay the borrowed amount, plus interest if you don't pay in full.

Personal Line of Credit

A type of loan that allows you to borrow money when needed, up to a certain limit. You pay interest only on the amount borrowed.

Home Equity Line of Credit

A loan that allows homeowners to borrow against the equity in their home with a variable interest rate based on the prime rate.

Closed-end credit involves borrowing a fixed amount and repaying it in equal instalments. Examples include personal loans, automobile loans, housing loans, and education loans.

Personal Loan

An unsecured loan that can be used for various personal expenses such as home renovations, wedding expenses, or debt consolidation.

Automobile Loan

A type of loan used to purchase a vehicle, typically secured by the car itself. The borrower makes monthly payments until the loan is paid off.

Housing Loan

A type of loan used to purchase or construct a home, secured by the property being purchased.

Education Loan

A type of financial assistance provided to help students pursue higher education. Repayment begins after graduation or when the student starts working.

4.2 Measuring Credit Capacity

Understanding Credit Capacity

Credit capacity refers to your ability to take on debt and repay it. Lenders evaluate your credit capacity to determine whether to approve your loan application and how much to lend you.

4.2.1 Debt Service Ratio (DSR)

Debt Service Ratio (DSR) is a ratio that measures the percentage of your income that goes to paying your debts. The lower your DSR, the higher your credit capacity and the lower your credit risk.

DSR Formula:

DSR = (Total Debt Payments / Gross Income) x 100

DSR Calculator:

Your Debt Service Ratio (DSR) is: 30%

Your DSR is good. Most lenders prefer a DSR below 40%, which indicates that you have sufficient income to cover your debt obligations.

4.2.2 The Concept of 5 C's

Lenders use the 5 C's framework to evaluate the creditworthiness of borrowers. The 5 C's are character, capacity, capital, collateral, and conditions.

1. Character

Do you pay bills on time? Your CCRIS payment report will tell the bank on your behalf.

2. Capacity

Can you repay the loan? Your Debt-to-Income ratio will do the math for you.

3. Capital

What assets or net worth currently you have?

4. Collateral

What property can you pledge to convince the loan provider that they can repossess should you default on the loan?

5. Conditions

What condition (e.g., economically) could affect your ability to pay back the loan?

DSR Example:

Gross Income: RM2,800

Car Loan: RM640

PTPTN: RM220

Total Debt: RM860

DSR Calculation:

DSR = (RM860 / RM2,800) x 100 = 30.71%

Most lenders in Malaysia set a threshold of 70%, which means you have a remaining 39.29% from your current income of RM2,800 (RM1,100) for future loan applications.

4.3 Maintaining Credit Scores and Ratings

Credit Scores in Malaysia

Credit scores are numerical representations of your creditworthiness. In Malaysia, your credit information is primarily managed through CCRIS (Central Credit Reference Information System) and CTOS (Credit Tip-Off Service).

4.3.1 CCRIS and CTOS

CCRIS

Central Credit Reference Information System (CCRIS) is a computerized database system operated by Bank Negara Malaysia. It processes credit-related data from financial institutions and synthesizes it into credit reports.

What CCRIS Reports Include:
  • Outstanding credits
  • Special attention accounts
  • Applications for credit

CTOS

Credit Tip-Off Service (CTOS) is an electronic 'archive' of information collated from various sources like gazettes, newspapers, court notices, and statutory bodies.

What CTOS Reports Include:
  • Bankruptcy information
  • Legal actions and summons
  • Company directorships
  • CTOS Score (ranges from 300 to 850)

It is important to know that both CCRIS and CTOS credit reports never blacklist the borrower. They only provide insights to help financial institutions in decision making.

4.3.2 Importance of Good Credit Records

Why Good Credit Records Matter

  • Access to Affordable Credit

    Good credit allows you to access affordable loans for major purchases like homes or cars.

  • Better Loan Terms

    A good credit score can lead to lower interest rates and better loan terms, saving you money.

  • Job Opportunities

    Some employers check credit reports as part of their hiring process, especially for finance-related positions.

How to Maintain Good Credit

  • Pay Bills on Time

    Late payments can negatively impact your credit score. Set up reminders or automatic payments.

  • Keep Credit Card Balances Low

    High utilization rates can lower your credit score. Aim to use less than 30% of your available credit.

  • Regularly Check Your Credit Reports

    Monitor your CCRIS and CTOS reports for any errors or fraudulent activities.

4.4 Avoiding and Correcting Credit Mistakes

Credit Mistakes

Credit mistakes can have serious consequences for your financial health and well-being. They can lower your credit score, increase your interest rates, limit your access to credit, and even affect your employment opportunities.

4.4.1 Loans Problem and Solutions

Common Loan Problems

  • Taking on too much high-interest debt
  • Missing loan payments or paying late
  • Paying only the minimum on credit cards
  • Not having a budget to manage debt
  • Borrowing for non-essential expenses

According to Bank Negara Malaysia (2021), the household debt-to-GDP ratio in Malaysia was 93.3% as of June 2021, which was higher than the pre-pandemic level of 82.9% in 2019.

Effective Solutions

  • 1

    Create a Budget

    Track your income and expenses to live within your means and avoid overspending.

  • 2

    Pay More Than the Minimum

    This helps reduce debt faster and save on interest charges.

  • 3

    Use Debt Snowball/Avalanche Method

    Prioritize debts by either smallest balance first (snowball) or highest interest rate first (avalanche).

  • 4

    Consider Debt Consolidation

    Combine multiple debts into one loan with a lower interest rate and a fixed repayment term.

  • 5

    Seek Professional Help

    Consult a debt management agency like AKPK (Credit Counselling and Debt Management Agency) for assistance.

Debt Repayment Strategies

Debt Snowball Method

This method involves paying off debts in order from smallest to largest balance, regardless of interest rate.

How it works:
  1. List all your debts from smallest to largest balance
  2. Make minimum payments on all debts
  3. Put extra money toward the smallest debt
  4. Once the smallest debt is paid off, add that payment to the next smallest debt
  5. Continue this process until all debts are paid off

Psychological advantage: Quick wins keep you motivated

Debt Avalanche Method

This method involves paying off debts in order from highest to lowest interest rate.

How it works:
  1. List all your debts from highest to lowest interest rate
  2. Make minimum payments on all debts
  3. Put extra money toward the highest-interest debt
  4. Once the highest-interest debt is paid off, add that payment to the next highest-interest debt
  5. Continue this process until all debts are paid off

Financial advantage: Saves the most money on interest in the long run

4.4.2 Bankruptcy in Malaysia

What is Bankruptcy?

Bankruptcy is a legal process that allows you to discharge some or all of your debts if you are unable to pay them.

Negative Impacts:
  • Losing your assets such as home, car, or savings
  • Damaging your credit record for up to 10 years
  • Restricting your financial freedom for up to 5 years

Alternatives to Bankruptcy

Voluntary Arrangement

An agreement between you and your creditors to repay your debts in full or in part over a period. Requires 75% creditor approval and court authorization.

Debt Management Programme

A plan offered by AKPK that allows you to consolidate your debts into one monthly payment with lower interest rates and longer repayment terms.

Individual Voluntary Arrangement

Similar to Voluntary Arrangement but involves appointing an insolvency practitioner to supervise the process. Also requires 75% creditor approval.

According to AKPK, as of June 2021, they have enrolled more than 300,000 Malaysians in the DMP and helped them to repay more than RM9 billion of debts.
