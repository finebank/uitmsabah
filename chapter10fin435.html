Banking Regulation and Management

This chapter covers key aspects of banking operations and regulatory frameworks that ensure stability and efficiency in the banking sector.

Key Topics Covered:

  • Capital adequacy framework and Basel Accords
  • Liquidity management strategies
  • Asset and liability management
  • Bank credit process and loan management
  • Problem loan detection and recovery techniques
  • Performance evaluation metrics for banks

Bank Manager's Primary Concerns

  1. Sufficient cash or liquid assets to meet deposit outflows (liquidity management)
  2. Acquiring assets with low risk of default and for diversification (asset management)
  3. Acquire funds by accepting deposits at low cost (liability management)
  4. Maintaining right level of capital protection against risk (capital adequacy management)

Capital Adequacy Framework

Capital adequacy refers to the amount of capital a bank or financial institution needs to hold as required by its financial regulator. These requirements are put in place to ensure that these institutions do not take on excessive leverage and become insolvent.

BNM's Objectives on Capital Ratio

  1. To protect depositors and their deposits
  2. To ensure a stable financial system
  3. To ensure a proper structure in the financial system

Liquidity Management

Liquidity management refers to a bank's ability to generate cash quickly to meet financial obligations. Banks need sufficient liquid assets to meet deposit withdrawals and other financial commitments.

Sources of Liquidity

New Deposits

Customer deposits provide a primary source of liquidity for banks.

Sale of Assets

Selling short-term securities to generate immediate cash.

Maturity of Assets

Cash flows from maturing loans and investments provide liquidity.

Borrowed Funds

Borrowing from other institutions or central banks for liquidity needs.

Basel III Liquidity Requirements

Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)

Ensures banks have enough high-quality liquid assets to survive a 30-day stress scenario. The ratio must be at least 100%.

LCR = High Quality Liquid Assets / Total Net Cash Outflows over 30 days ≥ 100%

Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)

Promotes longer-term funding stability by requiring banks to fund their activities with sufficiently stable sources of funding. The ratio must be at least 100%.

NSFR = Available Stable Funding / Required Stable Funding ≥ 100%

Strategies for Liquidity Management

  • Holding Liquid Assets

    Maintain a portfolio of high-quality liquid assets that can be easily sold.

  • Stable Liabilities

    Acquire large volume of stable deposits and longer-term funding.

  • Credit Lines

    Maintain lines of credit with other financial institutions for emergency liquidity.

  • Market Risk Monitoring

    Track interest rate changes that could affect earnings and asset values.

Liquidity Stress Testing

Banks conduct regular stress tests to assess their resilience to liquidity shocks. These tests simulate scenarios such as:

  • • Significant deposit outflows
  • • Market-wide liquidity crisis
  • • Credit rating downgrade
  • • Loss of wholesale funding access

Liquidity Ratios for Performance Evaluation

Loans to Total Assets

Total Loans / Total Assets

Measures the percentage of a bank's assets tied up in loans. Higher ratios indicate lower liquidity.

Liquid Assets to Total Assets

Cash and Short-Term Securities / Total Assets

Indicates the proportion of assets that can be quickly converted to cash. Higher ratios indicate better liquidity.

Performance Evaluation of Banks

Bank performance evaluation involves analyzing financial statements and calculating various financial ratios to assess profitability, liquidity, asset quality, capital adequacy, and management soundness.

Profitability Ratios

  • Return on Assets (ROA): Net profit / Total assets
  • Return on Equity (ROE): Net profit / Shareholders' equity
  • Spread: Interest rate on loans - Interest rate on deposits
  • Net Interest Margin: (Interest income - Interest expenses) / Earning assets

Liquidity Ratios

  • Loans to Total Assets: Total loans / Total assets
  • Liquid Assets to Total Assets: Cash and short-term securities / Total assets
  • Loan to Deposit: Gross loan / Total customer deposits

Asset Quality Ratios

  • NPL Ratio: Non-performing loans / Net loans
  • Deposit to Shareholder Fund: Customer deposits / Shareholder equity

Capital Adequacy Ratios

  • Capital-to-Risk Weighted Assets: (Tier 1 + Tier 2) / Risk-weighted assets
  • Core Capital Ratio: Tier 1 capital / Risk-weighted assets

Analysis Interpretation

  • Very low NPL ratio of 0.4%, indicating high asset quality

  • Deposit to shareholders' fund ratio at 6.00 times shows moderate leverage
  • Administrative expenses represent 18.18% of total expenditures, which should be monitored

Recommendations

  • Maintain the strong asset quality practices that have resulted in low NPL ratio
  • Consider strategies to improve deposit growth to support future loan expansion
  • Monitor administrative expenses to ensure operational efficiency
  • Continue prudent capital management to maintain strong capital adequacy ratios
