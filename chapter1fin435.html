Introduction to Financial Systems

Financial systems are at the core of modern economies, facilitating the flow of money and resources between different economic actors. This chapter provides an overview of financial systems, their components, and how they function.

Components of Financial System

A financial system consists of several interconnected components that work together to facilitate the flow of funds in an economy. Let's explore each component in detail.

Money

Used to pay for purchases and store wealth.

Financial Instruments

Transfer resources from savers to investors and distribute risk.

Financial Markets

Allow buying and selling of financial instruments quickly and cheaply.

Financial Institutions

Provide access to markets and assess borrower creditworthiness.

Regulatory Agencies

Ensure safe and reliable operation of the financial system.

Central Bank

Monitors and stabilizes the economy through monetary policy.

Direct vs. Indirect Finance

Direct Finance

Digital

In direct finance, borrowers sell securities directly to lenders without intermediaries.

  • Borrower issues securities
  • Lender purchases securities directly
  • Often facilitated by investment banks/brokers

Indirect Finance

Digital

In indirect finance, financial intermediaries stand between lenders and borrowers.

  • Savers deposit funds with intermediaries
  • Intermediaries make loans to borrowers
  • Intermediaries transform assets and manage risk

Financial Instruments

Financial instruments are written legal obligations of one party to transfer something of value (usually money) to another party at a future date, under specified conditions.

Bank loans

Bonds

Home mortgages

Stocks

Asset-backed securities

Insurance contracts

Futures contracts

Options

Swaps

  • Standardization of terms - Use of standardized financial instruments with clear terms and conditions
  • Information communication - Summarize important details about the issuer to help investors make informed decisions
  • Liquidity - Ability to convert the instrument into cash quickly without significant loss of value
  • Risk-return profile - Different instruments carry varying levels of risk and potential return

Financial Markets

Financial markets are the places where financial instruments are bought and sold. They play a crucial role in allocating resources and determining asset prices.

Functions of Financial Markets

Market Liquidity

Ensure that owners of financial instruments can buy and sell them cheaply and easily.

Information

Pool and communicate information about the issuer of a financial instrument.

Risk Sharing

Provide individuals with a place to buy and sell risks, and/or share them with others.

Financial Institutions

Financial institutions are firms that provide access to financial markets for both savers and borrowers. They play a critical role in the efficient allocation of resources in the economy.

Roles of Financial Institutions

  • Reduce transactions costs

    Make buying and selling financial instruments more efficient and less expensive.

  • Reduce information costs

    Screen and monitor borrowers to reduce asymmetric information problems.

  • Offer long-term loans

    Provide financing for extended periods to support investments and growth.

  • Offer savers ready access to funds

    Allow depositors to withdraw funds when needed while still providing long-term financing.

  • Provide less risky financial instruments

    Transform high-risk assets into lower-risk instruments for savers.

Financial Institutions as Brokers

Act as intermediaries that facilitate the direct transfer of funds from savers to borrowers without transforming the nature of the claims.

Financial Institutions that Transform Assets

Take deposits or insurance premiums from savers and transform them into different types of assets (loans, bonds, stocks, real estate).

Types of Financial Institutions in Malaysia

Depository institutions accept deposits from savers and make loans to borrowers. They are the most visible financial intermediaries in the economy.

Types of Depository Institutions in Malaysia:

Regulation of the Financial System

Financial regulation plays a crucial role in maintaining stability, protecting consumers, and ensuring the efficient functioning of the financial system.

Why Regulate the Financial System?

To Protect Investors

Ensure investors receive fair treatment, accurate information, and protection from fraud and manipulation.

To Protect Against Monopolies

Prevent financial institutions from exploiting customers through monopolistic practices.

To Safeguard Financial Stability

Ensure the overall stability of the financial system and prevent systemic risks that could damage the economy.

Financial Regulation in Malaysia

In Malaysia, the regulation of depository institutions is overseen by Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) under several key laws:

Financial Services Act 2013 (FSA)

Governs conventional banks, focusing on consumer protection, financial stability, and systemic risk management.

Islamic Financial Services Act 2013 (IFSA)

Regulates Islamic banks, ensuring compliance with Shariah law while maintaining financial stability.

Development Financial Institutions Act 2002

Oversees the operations and governance of Development Financial Institutions (DFIs).

Deposit Insurance (PIDM)

Perbadanan Insurans Deposit Malaysia (PIDM) guarantees deposits up to RM250,000 per depositor per bank in case of bank failures.

Central Bank of Malaysia Act 2009

Governs the powers, functions, and governance structure of Bank Negara Malaysia as a central bank.

Financial Stability

What is Financial Stability?

Financial stability is a condition where the financial intermediation process functions smoothly and there is confidence in the operation of key financial institutions and markets within the economy.

Importance of Financial Stability

Financial stability is crucial to support the efficient allocation of resources and distribution of risks across the economy. It provides the foundation for sustainable economic growth.

Consequences of Financial Instability

Financial instability can lead to costly spillover effects to the economy and potentially trigger financial crises with adverse economic consequences, including recession, unemployment, and loss of wealth.

Central Bank Objectives

Central banks work to reduce the volatility of economic and financial systems by pursuing specific objectives:

Low and Stable Inflation

Maintain price stability to preserve purchasing power and create a predictable economic environment.

High and Stable Growth

Promote sustainable economic growth and high employment levels.

Stable Financial Markets

Ensure the stability of financial institutions and markets to prevent crises.

Stable Interest Rates

Minimize volatility in interest rates to provide certainty for borrowers and lenders.

Stable Exchange Rate

Maintain a stable currency value relative to other currencies.

Islamic Banking

Islamic banking operates according to Shariah principles, which prohibit interest (riba) and encourage profit-sharing arrangements.

Introduction to Islamic Banking

The need for Islamic banking arose due to the increasing desire among Muslims to lead their lives in accordance with Shariah principles. This includes economic activities such as banking and finance.

Key Principles

  • Prohibition of interest (riba)
  • Promotion of profit-sharing
  • Ethical investing and financing
  • Asset-backed transactions
  • Avoidance of uncertainty (gharar)
  • Prohibition of gambling (maysir)

Product Offerings

Islamic banks offer similar product lines as conventional banks, but structured differently:

  • • Deposits
  • • Investment accounts
  • • Financing
  • • Trade finance
  • • Card services
  • • Money market instruments

Did You Know?

Malaysia is recognized as a global leader in Islamic finance, with one of the most developed Islamic banking systems in the world. Bank Islam Malaysia Berhad (BIMB), established in 1983, was Malaysia's first Islamic bank.

Shariah Concepts in Islamic Banking

Mudharabah

Profit Sharing

Partnership where one party provides capital and the other provides expertise/management. Profits are shared according to an agreed ratio.

Musyarakah

Joint Venture

Partnership where all parties contribute capital. Profits are shared according to an agreed ratio, while losses are shared according to capital contribution.

Murabahah

Cost Plus

Sale with a declared profit margin. The bank purchases an asset and sells it to the customer at a higher price, with payment deferred.

Wakalah

Agency

Contract where one party appoints another to act on its behalf. The bank acts as an agent and charges a fee for its services.

Wadiah

Safekeeping

Contract where one party entrusts assets to another for safekeeping. Used for current and savings accounts.

Ijarah Thumma Al bai

Hire Purchase

Leasing contract followed by a sale. The bank leases an asset to a customer, who eventually purchases it at the end of the lease term.

Bai' Bithaman Ajil

Deferred Payment Sale

Sale with deferred payment. The bank buys an asset and sells it to the customer at a markup, with payment in installments.

Qardhul Hassan

Benevolent Loan

Interest-free loan given for welfare purposes. The borrower is only required to repay the principal amount.

Bai' al-inah

Sell and Buy Back

Arrangement where the bank sells an asset to a customer on deferred payment and then immediately buys it back for cash at a lower price.

Application of Concepts on Products and Services

  • Deposits-taking products

    Current and savings deposits under al-wadiah (guaranteed custody)

  • Investment deposits

    Under al-mudharabah (profit sharing)

  • Financing facilities

    Working capital financing under al-murabahah (cost-plus)

    House financing under bai'bithaman ajil (deferred payment sale)

    Leasing under al-ijarah

    Project financing under al-musyarakah (profit-loss sharing)
