What is an Offshore Market?

An offshore market is a location where financial products and services are offered by offshore companies that meet the needs of financial market participants in a low tax regime and moderately or lightly regulated jurisdictions.

Funds move actively between market participants (surplus and deficit units) in these markets.

According to the IMF, some of the leading offshore financial centers include the Bahamas, Bermuda, British Virgin Islands, Cayman Islands, Gibraltar, Luxembourg, Jersey, and Panama. Other notable centers are Switzerland, Hong Kong, Singapore, Japan, and the United States.

Key Characteristics of Offshore Financial Centers

Low Tax Environment

Offshore centers offer favorable tax rates, tax exemptions, or tax incentives to attract foreign investments and businesses.

Minimum Exchange Control

Few restrictions on currency exchange and capital movements, allowing for free flow of funds across borders.

Many Financial Institutions

High concentration of banks, insurance companies, and other financial service providers relative to the size of the local economy.

Maximum Confidentiality

Strong privacy laws and banking secrecy provisions, though increasingly balanced with international transparency requirements.

Excellent Infrastructure

Well-developed physical and digital infrastructure to support efficient financial operations and transactions.

Stable Legal Framework

Clear and stable business-friendly legal systems with professional services to support business activities.

Malaysia's Offshore Market

Malaysia has established the Labuan International Business and Financial Center (Labuan IBFC) as its offshore financial center. Regulated by the Labuan Financial Services Authority (Labuan FSA), it offers a wide range of financial services with attractive tax incentives.

Labuan International Business and Financial Center (IBFC)

The Labuan International Business and Financial Center (IBFC) is Malaysia's offshore financial center established to provide a wide spectrum of financial services and products for corporations and individuals.

Located on Labuan Island off the coast of Borneo in East Malaysia, it was established in 1990 as part of Malaysia's efforts to develop a regional financial hub.

Labuan IBFC offers a dual financial system, combining both conventional banking and Islamic finance services, making it unique among offshore centers.

No Cash Transactions

Labuan Financial Services Authority (FSA)

The Labuan Financial Services Authority (Labuan FSA) was established on February 15, 1996, under the Labuan Financial Services Authority Act 1996.

It is the statutory body responsible for the development and administration of the Labuan International Business and Financial Centre (Labuan IBFC).

Labuan FSA ensures that the Labuan IBFC adheres to international standards and best practices, particularly those set by the Organization of Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD).

Establishment Timeline

1990

Labuan was established as an International Offshore Financial Centre (IOFC) with the enactment of tax and company laws.

February 15, 1996

Labuan Financial Services Authority (Labuan FSA) was established under the Labuan Financial Services Authority Act 1996.

2008

Rebranding from Labuan IOFC to Labuan International Business and Financial Centre (Labuan IBFC).

2010

New legislation framework was introduced to modernize and streamline the legal provisions.

Objectives of Labuan FSA

Promotion & Development

Promote and develop Labuan as an international center for business and financial services.

Policy Development

Develop national objectives, policies, and priorities for orderly development and administration of international business and financial services in Labuan.

Central Regulation

Act as a central regulator, supervisory and enforcement authority of the international business and financial services in Labuan.

Compliance Oversight

Prudential Supervision

Takes active measures to prevent terrorism financing activities in line with international standards and requirements.

Labuan Tax Structure

The tax system in Labuan is designed to be competitive and attractive for international businesses, while also complying with international standards and regulations.

Companies registered in Labuan can benefit from doing business in a low-cost and low-tax environment, making it an attractive jurisdiction for various business activities.

The tax framework is primarily governed by the Labuan Business Activity Tax Act 1990 (LBATA), which has undergone various amendments to stay current with international tax standards, including those set by the Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD).

Categories of Tax Treatment

3% Tax Rate

Offshore Trading Activities

Includes banking, insurance, trading, management, licensing and other active business operations.

Examples of Trading Activities:
  • Banking services
  • Insurance operations
  • Trading activities
  • Management services
  • Licensing of intellectual property
Tax Rate:

3% of audited net profits, provided the entity complies with tax substantial activity requirements.

0% Tax Rate

Offshore Non-Trading Activities

Refers to activities related to holding investments in securities, stocks, shares, loans, deposits, and immovable properties by an offshore company on its own behalf.

Examples of Non-Trading Activities:
  • Holding investments in securities
  • Holding shares in other companies
  • Maintaining loans and deposits
  • Owning immovable properties
  • Passive investment holding
Tax Rate:

0% (tax exempt), provided the entity complies with tax substantial activity requirements.

2019 Tax Regime Changes

The Labuan tax regime underwent significant changes effective January 1, 2019, to align with new international tax standards set by the OECD:

  • Abolished the flat tax rate of RM20,000 that was previously an option
  • Introduced the 3% rate on net profits for trading activities
  • Maintained tax exemption for non-trading activities
  • Introduced the requirement for "substantial activities" to qualify for tax benefits

Offshore Banking

Labuan banking means the business of receiving deposits on current accounts, deposit accounts, savings accounts, and includes investment banking business, Labuan financial business, Islamic banking business and other transactions specified by Labuan FSA.

Key Features:

  • Transactions can be done in any currency including Malaysian Ringgit where permitted by Bank Negara Malaysia
  • Dual financial system offering both conventional banking and Islamic finance
  • No cash transactions allowed in Labuan IBFC
  • Labuan investment banks are not allowed to accept deposits

Banking Services:

  • Project and infrastructure funding
  • Structured borrowings
  • Trade and export financing
  • Treasury services
  • Financial advisory
  • Digital banking services

Note: Banks can operate as a branch or subsidiary and can establish offices in other parts of Malaysia to facilitate their businesses. More than 50 banks operate in Labuan IBFC, with the majority belonging to international banking groups from the Asia Pacific region.

Offshore Insurance and Reinsurance

An insurance business transacted in foreign currency including life, general, reinsurance, captive insurance, insurance manager, underwriting manager and insurance broking, but does not include domestic insurance business.

Regulatory Framework:

  • Regulated under the Labuan Financial Services and Securities Act 2010
  • License required from the Ministry of Finance
  • Governed by Labuan laws including Labuan Islamic Financial Services and Securities Act 2010 for Islamic insurance

Types of Insurance Business:

  • Life insurance
  • General insurance
  • Reinsurance
  • Captive insurance
  • Insurance management
  • Underwriting management
  • Insurance broking

Note: Offshore insurance business may be operated by a branch/subsidiary of foreign insurers or Malaysian insurers with a license obtained from the Ministry of Finance. All transactions must be conducted in foreign currency.

Offshore Trust Companies

Trust companies play an important role in facilitating investors to incorporate or register their companies/business in Labuan IBFC and provide a diverse range of trust and corporate secretarial services.

Services Provided:

  • Company secretarial services
  • Resident directorships
  • Audit and accounting services
  • Company administration
  • Trusteeship for foreign denominated bond issuances
  • Administrative services for mutual funds
  • Administrative services for non-Labuan IBFC entities

Governing Legislation:

  • Labuan Financial Services and Securities Act 2010
  • Labuan Islamic Financial Services and Securities Act 2010
  • Labuan Companies Act 1990
  • Labuan Business Activity Tax Act 1990
  • Labuan Trust Act 1996

Note: Companies are registered under the Labuan Companies Act 1990. Trust companies must obtain the necessary licenses to operate and are subject to the oversight of Labuan FSA.

Offshore Fund/Asset Management

Labuan IBFC provides a wide array of private wealth management services that are especially suitable for family offices and wealth managers in facilitating dynamic wealth transfer, dynastic planning, and inheritance management.

Types of Funds:

  • Private funds: Shares not offered to the general public, owned by not more than 100 investors
  • Public funds: Funds offered to the general public with no restriction on the number of investors

Key Features:

  • Available through conventional or Islamic structures
  • Competitive fee structure
  • Flexible investment policies
  • Strong legal protection
  • Variety of fund structures available

Note: Asset management structures can be undertaken either via conventional or Islamic means, providing flexibility to meet various cultural and religious preferences of investors.

General Requirements for Business Activities

Companies carrying out lawful business in, from, or through Labuan must use currencies other than Malaysian Ringgit (RM), unless specifically permitted by Bank Negara Malaysia.

License is required for companies that intend to involve in specific businesses such as banking, insurance, fund management, leasing, factoring, and company management.

Companies must comply with anti-money laundering and counter-terrorism financing regulations.

Annual reporting and audit requirements must be fulfilled as per the regulations set by Labuan FSA.

Case Study: Offshore Banking Services

Scenario

ABC International Ltd, a multinational corporation based in Singapore, is considering setting up operations in Labuan IBFC to manage its regional treasury functions. The company plans to conduct various banking transactions and needs to understand the regulatory and tax implications.

Key Considerations:

  • ABC International wants to conduct transactions in various currencies
  • The company wants to minimize its tax liability
  • They need to understand the regulatory requirements in Labuan
  • They plan to have some transactions with Malaysian companies

Questions:

1. What type of banking license would be most appropriate for ABC International, and what are the key regulatory requirements they would need to comply with?
Answer: ABC International would likely need a Labuan investment banking license as they primarily deal with treasury functions rather than retail banking. They would need to comply with the Labuan Financial Services and Securities Act 2010, anti-money laundering regulations, and maintain proper accounting records. They must also ensure they have adequate capital based on Labuan FSA requirements and appoint qualified directors and management.
2. What would be the tax implications for ABC International's operations in Labuan, and how would transactions with Malaysian entities be treated?
Answer: ABC International would be subject to a 3% tax on its audited net profits for trading activities, provided it meets substantial activity requirements. For transactions with Malaysian entities, there are restrictions on tax deductions: interest payments (67% deductible), lease rentals (67% deductible), other payments (3% deductible). This means Malaysian companies dealing with ABC International would have limited tax deductions for payments made to them.
3. What are the key advantages and potential limitations of establishing in Labuan IBFC versus another offshore financial center?
Answer:
Advantages: Favorable tax regime, dual financial system (conventional and Islamic), strategic location in Asia-Pacific, well-regulated environment adhering to international standards, and access to ASEAN markets.
Limitations: Restrictions on tax deductions for Malaysian entities, no cash transactions permitted, primarily non-ringgit transactions, and the need to comply with substantial activity requirements to benefit from the tax incentives.
