What are Derivatives?

A derivative is a financial security whose payoff is linked to another security (the underlying asset).

Derivative securities generally involve an agreement between two parties to exchange a standard quantity of an asset or cash flow at a pre-determined price and at a specified date in the future.

If the value of underlying security changes, the value of the derivative security will change too.

Derivatives derive their value from the performance of an underlying entity

Types of Derivatives in Malaysia

Exchange-traded derivatives (ETD)

  • Contracts traded on derivatives exchanges
  • Standardized contracts
  • Exchange acts as the counterparty to all contracts
  • Includes options and futures contracts

Over-the-counter derivatives (OTC)

  • Privately negotiated contracts between two parties
  • Flexible terms that can be customized
  • Meet specific needs of parties involved
  • Not traded on a formal exchange

Underlying Assets

Financial Derivatives

  • Bonds, government bonds (3-, 5-, and 10-year bonds)
  • Short-term interest rate instruments on money market

Commodity Derivatives

  • Agricultural items (crude palm oil)
  • Precious metals (gold)

Futures Contracts

A futures contract is an agreement to transact involving the future exchange of a set amount of assets for a price that is settled daily.

Futures contracts are traded on a regulated exchange (Bursa Malaysia Derivatives), making them standardized and more transparent.

Futures contracts have lower counterparty risk, as the exchange and a clearinghouse act as intermediaries, ensuring both parties meet their obligations.

Key Features of Futures Contracts

Standardized

Standard contract specifications (size, expiry date, settlement, contract month)

Obligation

Both buyers and sellers have an obligation to fulfill the contract terms

Mark-to-Market

Daily settlement of profits and losses based on market price changes

Settlement

Can be settled physically (delivery of asset) or with cash (price difference)

Leverage

Allows traders to control a large position with a small margin deposit

Exchange Traded

Traded on an organized exchange with standardized terms

Clearinghouse

The clearinghouse becomes the buyer for every seller and the seller for every buyer, guaranteeing all transactions and reducing counterparty risk.

Margin Requirements

Initial Margin

Initial deposit required to open a futures position:

  • FKLI: Around RM4,200–RM5,000 per contract
  • FCPO: Around RM4,000–RM5,000 per contract
  • Set by Bursa Malaysia Derivatives Clearing
  • May be adjusted based on market volatility

Maintenance Margin

Minimum account balance that must be maintained:

  • If account falls below maintenance margin, investor receives margin call
  • Must deposit additional funds to bring balance back to initial margin
  • If margin is not maintained, broker closes out position
  • Typically set at 75-80% of the initial margin

Types of Traders

Speculators

  • Buy hoping prices will rise
  • Sell hoping prices will fall
  • Aim to profit from price movements
  • Don't intend to take delivery

Hedgers

  • Buy to protect against rising prices
  • Sell to protect against falling prices
  • Aim to reduce risk
  • Often have exposure to the underlying asset

Arbitrageurs

  • Take positions in multiple markets
  • Exploit price discrepancies
  • Low risk, low return strategy
  • Help maintain efficient markets

Options Contracts

An option is a contract that gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell an underlying asset at a pre-specified price for a specified time period.

Options can be classified as either call options or put options.

The premium limits the buyer's potential loss in the options market to the amount of the premium paid.

Call vs. Put Options

Call Option

A call option gives the buyer (holder) the right to buy an underlying asset at a specified price within a specific time period.

Call Option Buyer:
  • Pays premium
  • Has the right to buy
  • Expects price to rise
  • Limited risk (premium)
  • Unlimited potential gain
Call Option Seller:
  • Receives premium
  • Has obligation to sell
  • Expects price to fall or stay flat
  • Limited potential gain (premium)
  • Unlimited potential risk

Call Option Payoff Diagram

Put Option

A put option gives the buyer (holder) the right to sell an underlying asset at a specified price within a specific time period.

Put Option Buyer:
  • Pays premium
  • Has the right to sell
  • Expects price to fall
  • Limited risk (premium)
  • Potential gain limited to strike price
Put Option Seller:
  • Receives premium
  • Has obligation to buy
  • Expects price to rise or stay flat
  • Limited potential gain (premium)
  • High potential risk if price falls to zero

Option Styles

American Option

Can be exercised at any time from the date of purchase up until (and including) the expiry date.

European Option

Can be exercised only on the specified expiry date.

Option Status

In-the-money

Option has intrinsic value:

  • Call option: Market price > Strike price
  • Put option: Market price < Strike price

At-the-money

Option has no intrinsic value:

  • Market price = Strike price
  • For both call and put options

Out-of-the-money

Option has no intrinsic value:

  • Call option: Market price < Strike price
  • Put option: Market price > Strike price

Option Premium Components

Intrinsic Value

The amount by which an option is in-the-money:

  • Call option: Max(0, Market price - Strike price)
  • Put option: Max(0, Strike price - Market price)
  • Represents the profit if exercised immediately

Time Value

The additional value beyond intrinsic value:

  • Time value = Option premium - Intrinsic value
  • Represents the possibility of becoming more profitable before expiration
  • Decreases as expiration approaches (time decay)

Option Premium Formula:

Option Premium = Intrinsic Value + Time Value

Examples & Illustrations

Call Option Example

You have purchased a European call option contract on J&J common stock with the following details:

  • Exercise price: RM89
  • Current stock price: RM90.43
  • Option premium: RM2.17 per contract

Scenario 1: Stock rises to RM94

Payoff = Stock price - Strike price

Payoff = RM94 - RM89 = RM5

Net Profit = Payoff - Premium

Net Profit = RM5 - RM2.17 = RM2.83

Scenario 2: Stock falls to RM89.50

If exercised:

Net profit = RM89.50 - RM89 - RM2.17 = -RM1.67

If not exercised:

Net profit = -RM2.17 (premium paid)

Result: Better to exercise (-RM1.67 > -RM2.17)

Put Option Example

You have purchased a European put option contract on KK common stock with the following details:

  • Exercise price: RM95
  • Current stock price: RM96.18
  • Option premium: RM1.25 per contract

Scenario 1: Stock falls to RM93

Payoff = Strike price - Stock price

Payoff = RM95 - RM93 = RM2

Net Profit = Payoff - Premium

Net Profit = RM2 - RM1.25 = RM0.75

Scenario 2: Stock price doesn't change

If exercised:

Net profit = RM95 - RM96.18 - RM1.25 = -RM2.43

If not exercised:

Net profit = -RM1.25 (premium paid)

Result: Better not to exercise (-RM1.25 > -RM2.43)

Concept Check

1. What does a futures contract allow a buyer to do?

Correct! A futures contract is an agreement between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a specified future date.
Incorrect. A futures contract allows the buyer to buy or sell an asset at a future date at a price agreed upon today.

2. What is meant by margin in a futures contract?

Correct! Margin is the amount of money required to open and maintain a futures position, serving as a good faith deposit to ensure contract obligations are met.
Incorrect. Margin is the amount of money required to open and maintain a futures position.

3. What is a call option?

Correct! A call option gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy an underlying asset at a specified price within a specific time period.
Incorrect. A call option gives the holder the right to buy an asset at a specified price before a certain date.

True/False Questions

1. Derivatives are financial contracts whose value is derived from the value of an underlying asset.

Correct! Derivatives derive their value from an underlying asset.
Incorrect. Derivatives do derive their value from an underlying asset.

2. A call option gives the holder the right to buy an asset at a specified price before the option expires.

Correct! A call option gives the holder the right to buy an asset at a specified price.
Incorrect. A call option does give the holder the right to buy an asset at a specified price.

3. The premium paid for an options contract is refunded if the option is not exercised.

Correct! The premium is not refunded if the option is not exercised. It's the price paid for the right to exercise the option.
Incorrect. The premium is not refunded if the option is not exercised.

Problem-Based Question

FKLI Futures Problem

You plan to long one FKLI futures contract with the following details:

  • Current FKLI Index Price: 1,600
  • Futures Contract Price: 1,610
  • Contract Size: 50
  • Initial Margin Requirement: RM5,000
  • Maintenance Margin Requirement: RM3,000

a. Calculate the total value of the futures contract at the current futures price.

Hint: Multiply the Futures Contract Price by the Contract Size.
Solution: Total Value = Futures Contract Price × Contract Size = 1,610 × 50 = RM80,500

b. If the FKLI index price drops to 1,580 at the contract expiration, calculate the payoff from the futures contract.

Hint: For a long position, the payoff is (Final Price - Entry Price) × Contract Size.
Solution: Payoff = (Final Price - Entry Price) × Contract Size = (1,580 - 1,610) × 50 = -30 × 50 = -RM1,500
