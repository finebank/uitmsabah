Banking Products and Services Overview

Banks offer a wide range of products and services designed to meet the financial needs of individuals, businesses, and institutions. Understanding these offerings is crucial for making informed financial decisions.

These products and services can be broadly categorized into deposits, loans and advances, card services, electronic banking, and specialized financial services.

This module will explore these key product categories, their features, benefits, application processes, and how they compare with one another.

Main Categories of Banking Products

Deposit Products

Safe places to store money while potentially earning interest. Includes savings accounts, current accounts, and fixed deposits.

Loans & Advances

Financial products that provide funds for various purposes, such as home purchases, personal expenses, and business operations.

Card Services

Plastic or digital payment methods including credit cards, charge cards, and debit cards that facilitate transactions and provide other benefits.

Electronic Banking

Digital services that allow customers to conduct banking activities online, including internet banking, mobile banking, and payment systems.

Specialized Services

Additional financial services such as wealth management, trade finance, and foreign exchange that cater to specific customer needs.

Islamic Banking

Shariah-compliant financial products and services that adhere to Islamic principles, providing alternatives to conventional banking products.

Banking Products Ecosystem

Understanding the Ecosystem

Banking products and services form an interconnected ecosystem that supports the financial needs of various stakeholders in the economy.

Deposits provide the funding base for banks to offer loans and other financial products, while payment systems facilitate the movement of funds.

With advances in technology, this ecosystem continues to evolve, with digital banking playing an increasingly important role.

Key Terms to Know

Commercial Banks

Financial institutions that accept deposits, provide loans, and offer various financial services to individuals and businesses.

Interest Rate

The cost of borrowing money or the return earned on deposits, usually expressed as an annual percentage of the principal amount.

PIDM

Perbadanan Insurans Deposit Malaysia, an agency that protects depositors against the loss of their deposits in the event of a member bank failure, up to RM250,000 per depositor per bank.

Base Rate (BR)

A reference rate used by banks in Malaysia for pricing loans, replacing the Base Lending Rate (BLR) system. It reflects banks' cost of funds and other administrative costs.

Conventional vs. Islamic Banking

Malaysia has a dual banking system where conventional and Islamic banking operate in parallel. Understanding the differences between these two systems is essential for making informed financial decisions.

Important Note

In Malaysia, both conventional and Islamic banking products enjoy the same level of regulatory oversight from Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM). Islamic banking products are structured to provide similar financial outcomes as conventional products but through mechanisms that comply with Shariah principles.

Deposit Products

Deposits are the main source of funds for commercial banks. They represent excess funds placed by surplus units in the financial system for the purpose of investment or temporary placement.

Deposit products vary in terms of accessibility, interest rates, and features, catering to different financial needs and goals.

Banks offer different types of deposit accounts to meet various customer needs, including demand deposits (which can be withdrawn at any time) and time deposits (which have a fixed term).

Interest Calculation Example

For a 12-month fixed deposit of RM10,000 at 2.15% p.a.:

Specialized Deposit Products

Negotiable Instruments of Deposit (NID)

NIDs are deposit certificates issued by banks that can be bought and sold in the secondary market before maturity.

Key Features:

  • Transferable in the secondary market
  • Interest rates determined by interbank rates
  • Available in short-term (3-12 months) and long-term (1-5 years) options
  • Zero-coupon and floating rate variants available
  • Higher minimum investment amount compared to regular fixed deposits

NIDs combine the security of a bank deposit with the liquidity of a tradable instrument, making them attractive for investors who may need to cash out before maturity.

Specialized Target Group Deposits

Banks offer specialized deposit products tailored to specific customer segments with unique benefits and features.

Foreign Currency Deposits

Allows customers to save in foreign currencies (USD, EUR, GBP, etc.) while potentially benefiting from foreign exchange movements and international interest rates.

Premium Banking Deposits

Higher-interest deposit accounts for high-net-worth individuals who maintain substantial balances, often bundled with premium banking services and relationship management.

Goal-Based Savings Accounts

Specialized savings accounts designed to help customers save for specific goals like education, wedding, vacation, or home purchase, often with bonus interest for consistent deposits.

Education Savings Accounts

Long-term savings accounts specifically designed to help parents save for their children's education, sometimes offering tax advantages or matching contributions.

Deposit Protection in Malaysia

Perbadanan Insurans Deposit Malaysia (PIDM)

A government agency that administers the deposit insurance system, protecting depositors against the loss of their deposits in the event of a member institution's failure.

Key Aspects of PIDM Protection:

  • Automatic coverage for deposits in member banks
  • Protection up to RM250,000 per depositor per member bank
  • Separate coverage for conventional and Islamic deposits
  • No application required and no fees charged to depositors
  • Joint accounts are insured separately from individual accounts

Products Protected by PIDM:

  • Savings accounts
  • Current accounts
  • Fixed deposits / Term deposits
  • Foreign currency deposits
  • Islamic deposits based on Wadiah, Mudharabah, and Wakalah

Products NOT Protected by PIDM:

  • Unit trusts
  • Bond and debenture investments
  • Stocks and shares
  • Structured deposits and investment-linked products

If you have deposits exceeding RM250,000, consider spreading them across multiple banks to maximize PIDM protection. Remember that PIDM insurance is per depositor per member bank, so deposits in different banks are separately insured.

Loans and Advances

Loans and advances are arrangements where a borrower obtains financing from a lender with a promise to repay the borrowed funds at a later date, usually on an installment basis, with interest or finance charges.

From Bank Negara Malaysia's perspective, loans and advances are categorized into four main groups: public sector, private sector, other financial institutions, and foreign parties.

Understanding the different types of loans, their features, and application requirements is essential for making informed borrowing decisions.

Types of Loans and Advances

Home Loans

Financing for the purchase of residential properties, with the property serving as collateral.

  • Long-term (10-35 years)
  • Fixed or variable interest rates
  • Property as security

Personal Loans

Unsecured loans for personal purposes, such as education, emergencies, or major purchases.

  • Short to medium-term (6 months - 7 years)
  • Higher interest rates than secured loans
  • No collateral required

Business Loans

Financing for business operations, expansion, equipment purchase, or working capital.

  • Various terms based on purpose
  • Can be secured or unsecured
  • Multiple financing options available

Secured vs. Unsecured Loans

Secured Loans

Loans backed by collateral that the lender can claim if the borrower defaults.

Characteristics:
  • Lower interest rates due to reduced risk for lenders
  • Higher borrowing limits possible
  • Longer repayment terms
  • Risk of losing the collateral if you default
Examples:
  • Home loans/mortgages
  • Car loans
  • Fixed asset loans
  • Share margin financing

Unsecured Loans

Loans provided without collateral, based on the borrower's creditworthiness.

Characteristics:
  • Higher interest rates due to increased risk for lenders
  • Lower borrowing limits typically
  • Shorter repayment terms
  • No risk of losing specific assets, but legal action still possible
Examples:
  • Personal loans
  • Credit cards
  • Education loans
  • Unsecured business loans

Default Scenario Example

If you take a secured home loan of RM450,000 and fail to make payments, the bank can auction the property. If the auction only yields RM300,000, you would still be responsible for the remaining RM150,000 outstanding loan. Failure to settle this amount could result in bankruptcy proceedings.

Home Loans

Key Features

  • Used to finance the purchase of residential properties
  • Long repayment periods (10-35 years)
  • Secured by the property being purchased
  • Available with fixed or variable interest rates
  • Financing typically up to 90% of the property value
  • Monthly repayment should not exceed 1/3 of applicant's income

Additional Costs

  • Legal Fees: For sale & purchase agreement and loan documentation
  • Stamp Duty: Tax on property transfer (1-4% of property value) and loan agreement (0.5% of loan amount)
  • Valuation Fees: To assess the property's market value
  • Insurance/Takaful: Property insurance and mortgage reducing term assurance/takaful

Types of Home Loan Interest Rates

Fixed Rate

Interest rate remains constant throughout the loan term or for a specified period, providing predictable monthly payments.

Example: 4.5% p.a. fixed for 3 years
Variable/Floating Rate

Interest rate fluctuates based on changes in the bank's Base Rate (BR) plus a spread, potentially resulting in varying monthly payments.

Example: BR (3.0%) + 1.35% = 4.35% p.a.
Semi-Flexi Home Loan

Allows extra payments to reduce principal and interest, with the option to withdraw excess payments when needed.

Full-Flexi Home Loan

Functions like an overdraft account, where the loan is linked to a current account allowing flexible withdrawals up to the available limit.

Islamic Home Financing

Shariah-compliant alternatives to conventional home loans:

  • Musharakah Mutanaqisah (Diminishing Partnership): Bank and customer jointly own the property, with the customer gradually buying the bank's share over time.
  • Murabahah (Cost Plus): Bank purchases the property and sells it to the customer at a marked-up price, payable in installments.
  • Ijarah (Lease): Bank purchases the property and leases it to the customer, who eventually becomes the owner at the end of the lease term.

Business Loans and Overdraft Facility

Business Loans

Banks offer various financing options for businesses at different stages and with different needs.

Types of Business Loans:

Term Loan

A single loan or series of loans for a specified period, typically used for buying machinery, equipment, commercial property, or renovation. Assets purchased may serve as collateral.

Working Capital Loan

Short-term financing to cover day-to-day operational expenses such as inventory purchases, payroll, or rent during periods of lower revenue.

Trade Financing

Facilities for importers and exporters, including letter of credit, bankers' acceptance, shipping guarantee, and trust receipt.

Contract Financing

Financing based on specific contracts or purchase orders to help businesses fulfill large orders or contracts.

Overdraft Facility

An overdraft facility allows a current account holder to withdraw more than the available balance up to a pre-approved limit.

Key Features:

  • Only available for current account holders
  • Flexible borrowing up to a pre-approved limit
  • Interest charged only on the amount used
  • Typically used for working capital needs
  • Subject to periodic review
  • Commitment fee may apply on unused portion
Overdraft Interest Calculation Example

For an overdraft amount of RM20,000 used for 31 days in January at an interest rate of BR (4%) + 1.35% = 5.35% p.a.:

Interest = Overdrawn amount × Interest rate × Days used ÷ 365 days
= RM20,000 × 5.35% × 31 ÷ 365 days
= RM90.88

Credit Card

A payment card issued with a credit limit that allows cardholders to borrow funds to make purchases or withdraw cash.

A payment card that deducts money directly from the cardholder's bank account when making a purchase or withdrawal.

Key Features:
  • Linked directly to bank account
  • Immediate deduction of funds
  • No interest charges
  • ATM access for withdrawals
  • Limited or no rewards

Credit Cards in Detail

How Credit Cards Work

Credit cards provide a revolving line of credit that allows cardholders to make purchases up to a predetermined limit. The cardholder can either pay the full balance by the due date (avoiding interest) or make a minimum payment and carry the remaining balance forward (incurring interest).

1
Purchase Phase

Make purchases with your credit card up to your credit limit during the billing cycle (typically 30 days).

2
Statement Generation

At the end of the billing cycle, the bank generates a statement showing all transactions, the total amount due, minimum payment required, and the payment due date.

3
Grace Period

The period between the end of the billing cycle and the payment due date (typically 20-25 days) during which no interest is charged if the full balance is paid.

4
Payment Options

Pay the full balance (no interest), pay the minimum amount (interest on remaining balance), or pay any amount in between.

Types of Credit Cards

Rewards Credit Cards

Offer points for every ringgit spent, which can be redeemed for merchandise, gift cards, travel, or cash back.

Cashback Credit Cards

Return a percentage of your spending as cash credited to your account, often with higher rates for specific categories like groceries or petrol.

Travel Credit Cards

Earn air miles or travel points, offer travel-related perks like lounge access, travel insurance, or foreign transaction fee waivers.

Low Interest/Balance Transfer Cards

Feature lower interest rates or promotional 0% interest periods for balance transfers from other cards.

Secured Credit Cards

Requires a security deposit that typically equals the credit limit, designed for those with limited or poor credit history.

Islamic Credit Cards

Shariah-compliant alternatives to conventional credit cards, typically using structures such as:

  • Ujrah (Fee-based): The card issuer charges a fee for services rather than interest
  • Qard (Interest-free loan): The bank extends an interest-free loan to the cardholder
  • Tawarruq (Commodity Murabahah): A series of commodity transactions to create a credit facility

Credit Card Features and Costs

Common Benefits and Features

Interest-Free Period

Typically 20-25 days after the billing cycle to pay the full balance without incurring interest.

Cashback/Rewards

Earn points, miles, or cash back on purchases, often with bonus rates for specific spending categories.

Purchase Protection

Insurance coverage for purchases against damage or theft for a limited period after purchase.

Travel Benefits

Travel insurance, airport lounge access, rental car insurance, or concierge services on premium cards.

Installment Plans

Convert large purchases into fixed monthly installments, often with lower interest rates or even 0% interest.

Cost Considerations

Annual Fee

A yearly charge for card ownership, ranging from RM0 (free) to RM600+ for premium cards. Many banks waive the fee for the first year or based on spending thresholds.

Interest Rate (Finance Charge)

Applied to outstanding balances not paid in full, typically ranging from 15% to 18% per annum. Some banks use tiered rates based on payment history.

Cash Advance Fee

Charged when withdrawing cash using your credit card, usually 5% of the amount withdrawn or a minimum of RM15-RM20. Interest on cash advances often starts accruing immediately.

Late Payment Fee

Charged if payment is not made by the due date, typically RM10-RM100 depending on the outstanding balance.

Foreign Transaction Fee

Applied to purchases made in foreign currencies, usually 1-3% of the transaction amount in addition to the currency conversion rate.

Credit Card Interest Calculation Example

If you have an outstanding balance of RM5,000 with an interest rate of 18% p.a.:

Daily interest = (Outstanding balance × Interest rate per annum) ÷ 365 days
= (RM5,000 × 18%) ÷ 365
= RM2.47 per day

For a 30-day month, the interest charged would be approximately RM74.10 (RM2.47 × 30 days).

This illustrates why paying only the minimum amount (typically 5% of the outstanding balance) can lead to significant interest costs over time.

Debit Cards and Charge Cards

Debit Cards

Debit cards are linked directly to a bank account and deduct money immediately from that account when used for purchases or ATM withdrawals.

Key Advantages:

  • Spending limited to available funds, helping with budgeting
  • No interest charges or debt accumulation
  • Low or no annual fees
  • Combined functionality of ATM card and payment card
  • Less paperwork for application compared to credit cards

Considerations:

  • Limited or no rewards compared to credit cards
  • Less fraud protection in some cases
  • Some merchants (hotels, car rentals) may place holds on funds
  • No grace period for purchases

Charge Cards

Charge cards require full payment of the balance each month, with no option to carry a balance forward. They often have no preset spending limit, but this doesn't mean unlimited spending power.

Key Advantages:

  • No preset spending limit, offering flexibility for large purchases
  • Premium rewards and benefits (airport lounge access, concierge services)
  • No interest charges (since full payment is required)
  • Can help build financial discipline
  • Enhanced purchase and travel protections

Considerations:

  • Higher annual fees (typically RM250-RM1,000+)
  • Full payment required each month (no minimum payment option)
  • Strict penalties for late or missed payments
  • Higher approval requirements (income, credit history)
  • Limited availability in Malaysia (American Express, Diners Club)

Card Security and Best Practices

Protecting Your Card Information

Keep Your PIN Secret

Never share your PIN with anyone, including bank employees. Avoid using obvious PINs like birthdates or sequential numbers.

Cover the Keypad

When entering your PIN at ATMs or payment terminals, shield the keypad with your hand to prevent others from seeing it.

Secure Online Transactions

Only enter card details on secure websites (look for "https" and a padlock icon). Consider using virtual card numbers for online shopping if your bank offers them.

Enable Transaction Alerts

Set up SMS or email notifications for all card transactions so you can quickly identify unauthorized charges.

Keep Contact Information Updated

Ensure your bank has your current phone number and email address to contact you about suspicious activities.

What to Do If Your Card Is Lost or Stolen

1

Report Immediately

Contact your bank's 24-hour hotline as soon as you discover your card is missing to block the card and prevent unauthorized use.

2

Document the Report

Note down the date and time of your report, the name of the bank representative you spoke with, and any reference number provided.

3

Check Statements

Review your recent transactions to identify any unauthorized charges and report them to your bank.

4

File a Police Report

For cases of theft or suspected fraud, file a police report and provide a copy to your bank if requested.

5

Request a Replacement

Ask your bank to issue a replacement card with a new card number and PIN.

Smart Card Management Tips

Maximizing Rewards

  • Pay attention to rotating bonus categories
  • Redeem points strategically for maximum value
  • Check for promotions and special offers

Managing Card Costs

  • Negotiate annual fee waivers based on your usage

Building Credit Health

  • Check your CCRIS report regularly for accuracy

Electronic Banking

Electronic banking (e-banking) is a form of banking that uses digital technology to provide financial services, allowing customers to access their accounts and conduct transactions remotely without visiting a physical branch.

E-banking has revolutionized how customers interact with financial institutions, offering convenience, speed, and accessibility through various digital channels like internet banking, mobile apps, and ATMs.

As technology continues to evolve, e-banking services have expanded to include a wide range of features beyond basic account access, including investment services, loan applications, bill payments, and wealth management tools.

Security Measures in E-Banking

Bank-Side Security

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Requires multiple verification methods before granting access to accounts, typically combining something you know (password), something you have (mobile device), and something you are (biometrics).

Encryption

Data transmitted between your device and the bank is encrypted using protocols like Transport Layer Security (TLS), making it extremely difficult for attackers to intercept and understand the information.

Transaction Monitoring

Banks use advanced AI systems to monitor transactions in real-time for unusual patterns that might indicate fraud, triggering additional verification for suspicious activities.

Automatic Session Timeouts

Banking sessions automatically log out after a period of inactivity to prevent unauthorized access if you leave your device unattended.

User-Side Security Practices

1
Use Strong, Unique Passwords

Create complex passwords that mix letters, numbers, and symbols. Never use the same password for banking that you use for other websites.

2
Verify Website Security

Always check that the bank's website URL starts with "https://" and has a padlock icon in the address bar before entering any login information.

3
Use Secure Networks

Avoid accessing banking services using public Wi-Fi networks. Use your mobile data or a secure home network instead.

4
Enable Notifications

Set up SMS or email alerts for all transactions to quickly identify any unauthorized activity on your accounts.

5
Keep Devices Updated

Regularly update your devices, browsers, and banking apps to ensure you have the latest security patches and features.

Common E-Banking Threats to Watch For
  • Phishing: Fraudulent emails or messages that mimic your bank to trick you into revealing login credentials or personal information
  • SMS Spoofing: Text messages that appear to be from your bank but contain links to malicious websites
  • Keyloggers: Malware that records your keystrokes to capture passwords and other sensitive information
  • Man-in-the-Middle Attacks: Attackers intercept communication between you and your bank to steal information or modify transactions

Remember that banks will never ask for your full password, PIN, or one-time passwords through email, phone, or SMS. If you receive such requests, contact your bank directly through official channels.

Payment Systems

In Malaysia, payment systems consist of instruments, banking procedures, and interbank funds transfer systems that ensure and facilitate the circulation of money. These systems enable corporations, businesses, and consumers to transfer funds efficiently and securely.

Real-Time Electronic Transfer of Funds and Securities (RENTAS)

RENTAS is Malaysia's large-value payment system operated by Payments Network Malaysia Sdn Bhd (PayNet), a subsidiary owned by Bank Negara Malaysia.

Key Features:

  • Implemented in July 1999 to improve the efficiency of large value payments
  • 69 participants including commercial banks, Islamic banks, investment banks, and other financial institutions
  • No limit for transfers between members, minimum transaction of RM10,000 for non-RENTAS members
  • Operates on a real-time gross settlement basis, reducing settlement risk

Types of Transactions:

Interbank Funds Transfer System (IFTS)

Handles large-value interbank fund transfers and settlement of net positions from other payment systems.

Scripless Securities Transfer System (SSTS)

Facilitates the transfer and settlement of securities transactions on a delivery-versus-payment basis.

Retail Payment Systems

Retail payment systems handle everyday transactions between individuals and businesses, with various instruments and channels for different payment needs.

Payment Systems:

  • eSPCIK: Electronic clearing and settlement of cheques
  • Shared ATM Network: Allows customers to use ATMs of different banks
  • Interbank GIRO (IBG): Batch processing system for fund transfers
  • Financial Process Exchange (FPX): Online payment gateway for e-commerce
  • DuitNow: Instant fund transfers using mobile number or ID

Payment Instruments:

  • Cheques: Paper-based payment instruments
  • Credit/Debit/Charge Cards: Card-based payment methods
  • E-money: Digital stored-value instruments

Payment Channels:

  • ATMs: Self-service terminals for cash and basic banking
  • Internet Banking: Web-based banking platforms
  • Mobile Banking: Smartphone apps for banking services
  • Mobile Payments: Digital wallets and QR code systems

Digital Finance Innovations in Malaysia

Malaysia's financial sector has embraced digital transformation through various initiatives, guided by Bank Negara Malaysia's Financial Sector Blueprint (FSB) 2022-2026. The blueprint serves as a catalyst for digital innovation and economic transformation.

Strategic Thrusts for Digitalization

Funding Economic Transformation

Supporting innovative financing mechanisms and digital platforms to fund businesses and projects that drive economic growth and digital transformation.

Uplifting Financial Well-being

Enhancing financial accessibility and inclusion through digital solutions that improve the financial well-being of households and businesses.

Advancing Digital Financial Sector

Promoting digital adoption and innovation within the financial sector to enhance efficiency, reduce costs, and create new financial products and services.

Key Digital Finance Initiatives

Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC)

BNM is exploring the potential of CBDC, initially focusing on wholesale CBDC for cross-border payments. Project Dunbar, a partnership with the BIS Innovation Hub and central banks from Australia, Singapore, and South Africa, tests the use of multiple CBDCs and distributed ledger technology for international settlements.

Open Data Ecosystem

Developing policies and safeguards for responsible and ethical data usage in the financial sector. This includes establishing data standards, consent frameworks, and data rights to enable secure data sharing between financial institutions and fintech companies.

Fintech Support Ecosystem

Collaboration with agencies like MDEC and MRANTI to provide facilities and support for fintech startups. This includes regulatory sandboxes, funding opportunities, mentorship programs, and platforms for collaboration between established financial institutions and innovative startups.

Broader Digital Ecosystem

Integration of financial and non-financial infrastructures to create a comprehensive digital ecosystem. This includes digital identity solutions, seamless payment integrations, and interoperability between different financial platforms and services.

The Future of Digital Finance in Malaysia

Malaysia's digital finance landscape continues to evolve with a focus on balancing innovation with stability and security. Future developments will likely include expanded adoption of artificial intelligence in financial services, further integration of Islamic fintech solutions, enhanced cross-border payment systems, and more sophisticated digital financial inclusion initiatives to reach underserved populations.
