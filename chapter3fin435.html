Money Market

Definition & Characteristics

What is the Money Market?

The money market is a segment of the financial market where short-term debt instruments (with maturities of one year or less) are issued and traded.

It serves as a mechanism for short-term borrowing and lending, allowing market participants to manage their short-term liquidity needs.

Once issued, money market instruments trade in an active secondary market, providing liquidity to holders.

Key Characteristics

Money market instruments provide investment opportunities that offer:

  • Higher returns than cash - Better returns than simply holding physical cash
  • High liquidity - Can be easily converted to cash with minimal loss of value
  • Low default risk - Relatively safe compared to longer-term investments

Market Participants

Key Participants in the Malaysian Money Market

1
Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM)

Acts as the supervising and controlling authority. BNM plays a direct role in managing liquidity in the Malaysian banking system, controlling inflation, and influencing interest rates through money market operations.

2
Banking Institutions

Commercial banks and investment banks are major participants in the money market. They use it to manage their short-term liquidity needs, lend excess reserves, and trade various instruments.

3
Non-Bank Financial Institutions

Pension funds, unit trust funds, and insurance companies participate in the money market to manage their short-term cash positions and earn returns on temporarily idle funds.

4
Corporations

Multinational companies and small/medium enterprises use the money market to manage their cash flows, invest excess cash, and raise short-term funding through instruments like commercial paper.

5
Cagamas Berhad

The National Mortgage Corporation of Malaysia issues debt securities to fund the purchase of housing loans from financial institutions.

6
Money Brokers

Act as intermediaries to market participants, facilitating transactions between buyers and sellers in the money market.

Malaysian Money Market

The Malaysian Money Market comprises both conventional and Islamic markets. Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) uses the money market in implementing monetary policy, including money market operations.

BNM plays a direct role in managing liquidity in the Malaysian banking system by controlling inflation and influencing interest rates. To achieve this, various money market operations are implemented.

BNM's Money Market Instruments Include:

  • Bank Negara Monetary Notes (BNMN)
  • Malaysia Islamic Treasury Bills (MITB)
  • Repurchase Agreements (Repo)
  • Banker's Acceptances
  • Malaysia Treasury Bills (MTB)
  • Islamic Interbank Money Market

Money Market Risks & Management

Types of Risks

  • Credit Risk

    The risk that a borrower is unable to make payment on the maturity date agreed. Default payment can cause the other party to suffer losses.

  • Liquidity Risk

    The inability to convert assets to cash or inability to have access to funds when needed, potentially forcing the sale of assets at unfavorable prices.

  • Interest Rate Risk

    The risk that fluctuations in interest rates will lead to adverse value of assets, affecting the market value of money market instruments.

  • Operational Risk

    Risks arising from inadequate or failed internal processes, people, and systems, including bad systems and manpower problems.

Risk Management Strategies

  • Managing Credit Risk

    Conduct proper credit evaluation of counterparties, set exposure limits, diversify investments across different issuers, and monitor credit ratings regularly.

  • Managing Liquidity Risk

    Maintain a balance of short-term and long-term assets, establish liquidity contingency plans, and ensure access to multiple funding sources.

  • Managing Interest Rate Risk

    Develop tools to predict interest rate movements and maintain a "matched book" where asset durations match liability durations, and use hedging instruments when appropriate.

  • Managing Operational Risk

    Provide regular training to staff, implement robust internal controls, update systems regularly, and conduct periodic risk assessments.

Money Market Instruments

Money market instruments are short-term debt securities that enable borrowers to raise funds for short periods and allow lenders to earn returns on temporarily idle funds. Let's explore the main instruments.

Treasury Bills (T-bills)

Characteristics

  • Short-term obligations issued by the government (In Malaysia, known as Malaysia Government Securities or MGS)
  • Issued to cover current government budget deficits or to refinance maturing government debt
  • Issued on a discount basis (sold below face value and redeemed at face value)
  • Used as a main tool by BNM to implement monetary policy
  • Can be bought and sold in an active secondary market

Repurchase Agreements (REPO)

Characteristics

A REPO is an agreement involving the purchase of securities by one party (the repo buyer) from another party (the repo seller), with a promise to sell the securities back at a specified price on a specified date in the future.

  • Most REPOs are collateralized, meaning the seller holds securities to back the transaction
  • From the lender's perspective, the transaction is referred to as a "Reverse REPO"
  • REPOs function essentially as collateralized loans, where the securities serve as collateral
  • Common maturities range from overnight to a few weeks, though longer terms are possible
  • Central banks use REPOs as a tool for implementing monetary policy and managing liquidity

Other Money Market Instruments

Commercial Paper (CP)

  • Unsecured short-term promissory note issued by a company
  • Used to finance working capital requirements
  • Generally held by investors from issue until maturity
  • Maturities range from 1 to 270 days
  • Credit rating is important as CP is unsecured and not actively traded

Negotiable Certificates of Deposit (CD)

  • Bank-issued time deposit with specified interest rate and maturity date
  • Negotiable (can be sold) in the secondary market
  • Whoever holds the CD at maturity receives the principal and interest
  • Maturities up to 5 years for retail investors
  • Generally offers higher interest rates compared to fixed deposits
  • In Malaysia, known as Negotiable Instrument of Deposits (NIDs)

Banker's Acceptances (BA)

  • Time draft payable to a seller of goods, with payment guaranteed by a bank
  • Many arise from international trade transactions
  • Includes Letters of Credit (L/C) used to finance trade in goods
  • Foreign exporters often prefer banks as guarantors before shipping goods
  • Can be traded in secondary markets (payable to bearer at maturity)
  • Sold at a discount from face value

Foreign Exchange Market

Definition & Characteristics

What is the Forex Market?

The foreign exchange market (FX) is a market in which cash flows from the sale of products or assets denominated in foreign currency are transacted.

It is the largest and most liquid financial market in the world, with a daily trading volume exceeding $6 trillion.

The most actively traded currency pairs in the global FX market are EUR/USD, USD/JPY, and GBP/USD.

Exchange Rates

The foreign exchange rate is the price at which one currency can be exchanged for another currency.

Example:

1 USD = 4.3350 MYR

This means that 1 US Dollar can be exchanged for 4.3350 Malaysian Ringgit. If you go to a money exchange counter in Malaysia with 1 USD, you would get back RM4.3350 in return.

Participants in the Forex Market

1

Commercial Banks

Meet requirements of corporations, manage their own trading positions, and operate under regulatory authority. Commercial banks are the primary participants in the forex market.

2

Corporations

Pay exporters for the purchase of goods and services, manage currency risk for international operations, and convert profits earned in foreign currencies.

3

Non-Bank Financial Institutions

Investment firms, hedge funds, pension funds, and insurance companies participate in forex markets for investment purposes and to manage their international exposures.

4

Money Brokers

Act as middlemen in identifying buyers and sellers who have matching amounts to trade in foreign currencies. They earn income from commissions on transactions and interact with banks by providing "indication rates" and "dealing rates."

5

Bank Negara Malaysia

Ensures exchange rate stability, manages foreign reserves, intervenes in forex markets to reduce volatility by buying or selling large amounts of currencies, and implements forex policies such as fixed rate or managed floating exchange rate regimes based on economic conditions.

Foreign Exchange Transactions

There are two primary types of foreign exchange transactions: spot transactions and forward transactions. Each serves different purposes in the forex market.

Spot Transactions

Spot foreign exchange transactions involve the immediate exchange of currencies at the current or spot exchange rate. These transactions are typically settled within two business days.

Example:

You are a business owner in Malaysia, and you need to buy goods from a supplier in the United States. The supplier has quoted a price of USD 10,000 for the goods.

The current spot exchange rate is 1 USD = 4.60 MYR.

To pay the USD 10,000, you will convert Malaysian Ringgit (MYR) into US Dollars (USD) at the current spot rate.

Amount in MYR = USD 10,000 Ã— 4.60 MYR/USD = RM46,000

The exchange happens immediately, so you receive the USD 10,000 right away and the supplier receives their payment in USD.

Forward Transactions

Forward foreign exchange transactions involve the exchange of currencies at a specified exchange rate (or forward exchange rate) at some specified date in the future. They help manage currency risk from future transactions.

Example:

You are a Malaysian company that needs to pay USD 50,000 to a supplier in the United States, but the payment is due in three months.

The current spot exchange rate is 1 USD = 4.60 MYR, but you want to lock in a future exchange rate to avoid uncertainty due to potential currency fluctuations.

The forward exchange rate for a contract to be settled in three months is 1 USD = 4.65 MYR.

Amount in MYR = USD 50,000 Ã— 4.65 MYR/USD = RM232,500

When the payment is due in three months, you will exchange RM232,500 for USD 50,000 at the agreed forward rate of 4.65 MYR/USD, regardless of the prevailing spot rate at that time.

Foreign Exchange Risks

Primary Risks

  • Exchange Rate Risk

    The risk that the value of one currency relative to another will change, affecting the value of transactions or investments.

  • Interest Rate Risk

    Changes in interest rates affect exchange rates and the cost of borrowing in foreign currencies.

  • Credit Risk

    The risk that the counterparty in a forex transaction might default on their obligations.

  • Liquidity Risk

    The risk that a participant may not be able to buy or sell a currency quickly enough to prevent or minimize loss.

Additional Risks

  • Political Risk

    Changes in political conditions or government policies that can impact currency values and stability of the forex market.

  • Economic Risk

    Changes in inflation, economic growth, and trade balances can affect currency values and create volatility.

  • Operational Risk

    Risks arise from failures in internal processes, people, or systems in executing forex transactions.

  • Geopolitical Risk

    Events such as wars, conflicts, or international sanctions can cause significant currency fluctuations.

Bank Negara Malaysia's Role in Forex Market

Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) plays a critical role in the Malaysian foreign exchange market. BNM's functions in the forex market include market intervention, exchange rate management, and policy implementation.

Market Intervention

BNM intervenes in the forex market to reduce fluctuations and stabilize the ringgit to stem currency movements that are deemed excessive. This helps maintain stability in the financial system and supports economic activities.

Managed Float

BNM manages the floating exchange rate to avoid heavy trading that can cause volatility. The central bank closely monitors market conditions and intervenes by buying or selling currencies in the opposite direction of what the market is doing.

Exchange Rate Policy Implementation

BNM decides whether to implement policies on forex such as fixed rate or managed floating exchange rate regime depending on economic conditions. During the Asian Financial Crisis of 1997-1998, BNM implemented a fixed exchange rate regime to stabilize the economy.

Currently, Malaysia operates under a managed float regime, where BNM allows market forces to determine the value of the ringgit while intervening when necessary to reduce excessive volatility.
